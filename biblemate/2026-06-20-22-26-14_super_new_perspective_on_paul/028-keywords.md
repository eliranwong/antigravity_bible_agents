# Keywords and Theological Terminology Study

**Persona**: Oxford Bible Scholar  
**Date**: 2026-06-20  

This study examines the five central Greek terms/phrases at the heart of the New Perspective on Paul (NPP) debate, utilizing lexical data, morphology, and context from Phase 1.

---

## 1. ἔργα νόμου (*erga nomou* — Works of the Law)

### Original Language and Morphology
*   **Greek Lemma**: ἔργον (noun, neuter, plural) + νόμος (noun, masculine, singular, genitive).
*   **Transliteration**: *erga nomou*.
*   **Morphological Parsing**: Noun Direct Object / Genitive Modifier (see Gal 2:16, Rom 3:28).
*   **Strong's Number**: G2041 (*ergon*) + G3551 (*nomos*).

### Semantic Range
In Classical and Hellenistic Greek, *ergon* refers to deeds, actions, toil, or the product of work. *Nomos* generally denotes custom, law, or prescriptive usage. Extrabiblically, *erga nomou* is rarely attested in Greco-Roman literature. However, it appears in Jewish sectarian literature. A highly significant parallel was discovered in the Dead Sea Scrolls (4QMMT / *Miqsat Ma'ase ha-Torah*), translated as "some of the works of the law" (*ma'aseh ha-torah*). In 4QMMT, this phrase specifically refers to halakhic rulings concerning ceremonial purity, calendar regulations, and circumcision, designed to maintain the sectarian community’s separation from Gentiles and compromise.

### Contextual Meaning
In Galatians 2:16 and Romans 3:20, 28, the traditional Reformed view interprets *erga nomou* as all human efforts to obey the moral law of God (Torah) in order to earn salvation. 
In contrast, James Dunn and NPP scholars argue that in contexts like Galatians 2, where Paul is confronting Peter over table fellowship with Gentiles, *erga nomou* refers specifically to those laws that functioned as Jewish "boundary markers"—circumcision, sabbath observance, and dietary laws. In this context, *erga nomou* designates national identity markers that separated Jews from Gentiles.

### Theological Significance
Under the traditional view, *erga nomou* represents the legalistic attempt to establish self-righteousness, contrasting with the grace of the Gospel. Under the NPP, it represents ethnocentric exclusion, contrasting with the universal scope of the Abrahamic covenant. Both readings agree that *erga nomou* cannot justify, but they differ on the theological problem: is it human self-effort (legalism) or Jewish national pride (nationalism/covenantal exclusivism)?

## 2. δικαιοσύνη θεοῦ (*dikaiosynē theou* — Righteousness of God)

### Original Language and Morphology
*   **Greek Lemma**: δικαιοσύνη (noun, feminine, singular) + θεός (noun, masculine, singular, genitive).
*   **Transliteration**: *dikaiosynē theou*.
*   **Morphological Parsing**: Noun (nominative or accusative) + Genitive Modifier (see Rom 3:21-22).
*   **Strong's Number**: G1343 (*dikaiosynē*) + G2316 (*theos*).

### Semantic Range
In Classical Greek, *dikaiosynē* is the virtue of justice or righteousness (e.g., Plato’s *Republic*). In the Septuagint (LXX), it translates the Hebrew *tsedaqah* and *tsedeq*, which are covenantal terms referring to God's saving activity, faithfulness, and vindication of His people (e.g., Isa 51:5).

### Contextual Meaning
In Romans 3:21-26 and Philippians 3:9, the exact grammatical nature of the genitive (*theou*) is highly disputed:
1.  **Objective Genitive**: Righteousness before God, i.e., a status bestowed on believers by God (Luther, Calvin, traditional Protestantism).
2.  **Subjective / Possessive Genitive**: God’s own attribute of righteousness or faithfulness to His covenant promises (N.T. Wright, NPP).
3.  **Genitive of Origin**: A righteousness that comes *from* God (*dikaiosynē ek theou* as explicitly stated in Phil 3:9).

### Theological Significance
If *dikaiosynē theou* is God's covenant faithfulness (NPP), the doctrine of justification is centered on God's fidelity to His promises to Abraham to create a single, multi-ethnic family. If it is an imputed status (traditional), justification is primarily a forensic declaration of an individual's right standing before God based on the active and passive obedience of Christ.

## 3. πίστις Χριστοῦ (*pistis Christou* — Faith of/in Christ)

### Original Language and Morphology
*   **Greek Lemma**: πίστις (noun, feminine, singular) + Χριστός (noun, masculine, singular, genitive).
*   **Transliteration**: *pistis Christou*.
*   **Morphological Parsing**: Noun (nominative, dative, or genitive) + Genitive Modifier (see Gal 2:16, Rom 3:22, Phil 3:9).
*   **Strong's Number**: G4102 (*pistis*) + G5547 (*christos*).

### Semantic Range
*Pistis* carries the double meaning of "faith" (belief, trust) and "faithfulness" (fidelity, trustworthiness). 

### Contextual Meaning
The grammatical debate centers on whether the genitive *Christou* is objective or subjective:
1.  **Objective Genitive ("faith in Christ")**: Christ is the *object* of the believer's trust. This is the traditional reading (supported by the KJV's "faith of Christ" but functionally read as "faith in Christ", and modern versions like ESV2016 and NASB2020).
2.  **Subjective Genitive ("faithfulness of Christ")**: Christ's own *faithfulness* or obedience to God’s saving plan (especially on the cross) is the source of justification. This reading is preferred by many NPP scholars (and translated in the NET: "the faithfulness of Jesus Christ").

### Theological Significance
If *pistis Christou* is a subjective genitive, the basis of justification shifts from human belief to Christ's messianic obedience. Belief remains necessary, but the theological emphasis is placed on the redemptive-historical action of Christ rather than the cognitive or trust response of the individual.

## 4. νόμος (*nomos* — Law)

### Original Language and Morphology
*   **Greek Lemma**: νόμος (noun, masculine, singular/plural).
*   **Transliteration**: *nomos*.
*   **Strong's Number**: G3551.

### Semantic Range
In Greek literature, *nomos* means custom, law, rule, or principle. In Jewish-Hellenistic writing (LXX, Philo, Josephus) and the NT, it almost exclusively refers to the Torah—the Mosaic Law, including the Pentateuch or the entire Hebrew Scriptures.

### Contextual Meaning
Paul uses *nomos* in multiple senses:
1.  The concrete commands given at Sinai (Gal 3:17).
2.  A general principle or force (Rom 7:21).
3.  The Jewish scriptures as a whole (Rom 3:19).
In the NPP debate, the focus is on whether *nomos* is inherently good but temporary (NPP), or if it represents a legalistic system that provokes sin and was never meant to give life (traditional).

### Theological Significance
Understanding *nomos* affects how one views the continuity between the covenants. The NPP sees the law as a boundary marker that historically served a purpose but has now been set aside in Christ. The traditional view distinguishes between the moral law (eternally binding) and the ceremonial/civil law (fulfilled and abrogated).

## 5. δικαιόω (*dikaioo* — To Justify)

### Original Language and Morphology
*   **Greek Lemma**: δικαιόω (verb).
*   **Transliteration**: *dikaioō*.
*   **Morphological Parsing**: Present, Aorist, or Future tense, active or passive voice.
*   **Strong's Number**: G1344.

### Semantic Range
In Classical Greek, *dikaioo* means "to hold as right," "to do justice," or sometimes "to punish." In biblical Greek (LXX and NT), it corresponds to the Hebrew *hitsdiq* (hiphil of *tsadaq*), which is forensic (legal): "to declare righteous," "to vindicate," or "to acquit." It is distinct from making someone righteous (which would be a process of sanctification).

### Contextual Meaning
The debate is over *when* and *what* justification declares:
1.  **Traditional Reformed**: Justification is the forensic declaration that a sinner is righteous in God's sight, having the righteousness of Christ imputed to them. It is an individual soteriological transition.
2.  **New Perspective (Wright)**: Justification is not how one *becomes* a Christian, but the declaration that one *is* a member of the covenant family. It is ecclesiological and covenantal—an announcement of who belongs to God's family (Jew and Gentile alike).

### Theological Significance
This shift changes the primary question of justification from "How can I be saved from God's wrath?" (soteriological/vertical) to "Who are the people of God, and on what basis are they united?" (ecclesiological/horizontal).
