# Structural and Literary Outlines of Romans 1-8 and Galatians 1-4

**Persona**: Oxford Bible Scholar  
**Date**: 2026-06-20  

This outline traces the structural macro-structures and literary features of Romans 1-8 and Galatians 1-4, highlighting how the arguments flow toward the central justification texts.

---

## 1. Epistle to the Romans (Chapters 1-8)

### I. Introduction and Thematic Thesis (1:1-17)
*   **1:1-7 — Prescript and Apostolic Greeting**: A dense salutation that establishes Paul’s authority and the Christological center of his gospel (descended from David, declared Son of God by resurrection).
*   **1:8-15 — Thanksgiving and Paul’s Desire to Visit Rome**: Literary transition establishing a connection with the Roman community.
*   **1:16-17 — The Thematic Statement (Thesis)**: The gospel as the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, Jew first and also Greek, revealing the "righteousness of God" (*dikaiosynē theou*) from faith to faith.
    *   *Literary Note*: Quoting Habakkuk 2:4 acts as a structural anchor for the entire epistle's faith-argument.

### II. The Universal Need for Righteousness (1:18-3:20)
*   **1:18-32 — The Wrath of God against Gentile Ungodliness**: Focuses on idolatry and moral decay.
    *   *Literary Note*: Uses a diatribe-like style, describing a downward spiral of human degradation resulting from God "giving them up" (*paredōken*).
*   **2:1-16 — The Self-Righteous Jew and Impartial Judgment**: Addresses the imaginary Jewish interlocutor who judges the Gentile but does the same things. Establishes that God is impartial (2:11).
*   **2:17-29 — The Failure of Torah-Privilege and Circumcision**: Demolishes reliance on ethnic identity markers (circumcision, possession of Torah).
    *   *Literary Note*: Employs the rhetorical device of the diatribe, ending with a chiasm on inner vs. outer circumcision (2:28-29).
*   **3:1-8 — Jewish Advantage and God's Faithfulness**: Addresses objections regarding covenant faithfulness.
*   **3:9-20 — The Universal Guilt of Humanity**: Compilation of Old Testament proof texts (3:10-18, the "Scripture chain") proving all are under sin.
    *   *Literary Note*: The section ends with an *inclusio* with 1:18, confirming that by "works of the law" (*erga nomou*) no flesh will be justified.

### III. The Manifestation of Righteousness by Faith (3:21-4:25)
*   **3:21-26 — The Righteousness of God Revealed in Christ**: The heart of the letter. The righteousness of God has been manifested apart from the law, though attested by the Law and Prophets.
    *   *Literary Note*: A single complex Greek sentence in 3:21-26, rich in sacrificial (*hilastērion*) and forensic (*dikaioō*) vocabulary.
*   **3:27-31 — The Exclusion of Boasting**: Boasting is excluded by the "law of faith."
*   **4:1-12 — Abraham Justified by Faith before Circumcision**: Abraham as the test case. Faith was reckoned to him as righteousness (Gen 15:6) *before* he was circumcised (Gen 17).
    *   *Literary Note*: Uses Rabbinic *gezerah shawah* (linking Gen 15:6 and Ps 32:1-2 based on the common word "reckon" / *logizomai*).
*   **4:13-25 — The Promise Realized through Faith**: The promise to Abraham is for all his descendants, not just those of the Law, but those of his faith.

### IV. The Results of Justification (5:1-8:39)
*   **5:1-11 — Peace with God and Reconciliation**: The subjective and objective results of justification.
*   **5:12-21 — Adam and Christ Typology**: The representative heads of the old and new humanity.
    *   *Literary Note*: Parallel structure of comparison (*hosper... houtōs* - "just as... so also") contrasting Adam's trespass with Christ's act of righteousness.
*   **6:1-14 — Union with Christ and Deadness to Sin**: Baptism as burial and resurrection with Christ, defeating the objection that grace encourages sin.
*   **6:15-23 — Slavery to Righteousness**: Contrast between two slaveries (sin vs. obedience).
*   **7:1-6 — Released from the Law to Serve in the Spirit**: The marriage analogy illustrating release from the law's domain.
*   **7:7-25 — The Law and the Struggle with Sin**: The role of the law in exposing and provoking sin.
    *   *Literary Note*: Written in the first-person singular ("I" / *ego*), a highly debated rhetorical persona (the unregenerate Jew, Paul’s past, or the Christian struggle).
*   **8:1-17 — Life in the Spirit**: No condemnation for those in Christ Jesus; the law of the Spirit of life sets free.
*   **8:18-39 — Future Glory and the Inseparable Love of God**: Cosmic redemption, the golden chain of redemption (8:29-30), and the final hymn of assurance.

## 2. Epistle to the Galatians (Chapters 1-4)

### I. Introduction and Defense of Paul’s Gospel (1:1-2:14)
*   **1:1-5 — Greeting**: Unusually brief and direct greeting, establishing Paul’s non-human commission.
    *   *Literary Note*: Conspicuously lacks the standard thanksgiving section (*prooemium*), indicating extreme urgency.
*   **1:6-10 — The Galatian Apostasy**: Pronouncement of anathema on those preaching another gospel.
*   **1:11-24 — The Divine Origin of Paul’s Gospel**: Paul’s autobiography, from Pharisaic zealot to apostle.
*   **2:1-10 — Validation by the Jerusalem Pillars**: Paul’s meeting with Peter, James, and John, who confirmed his Gentile mission and did not compel Titus to be circumcised.
*   **2:11-14 — The Antioch Incident**: Confrontation with Peter over his withdrawal from table fellowship with Gentiles under pressure from the "circumcision party."

### II. The Theological Core: Justification by Faith (2:15-21)
*   **2:15-21 — Jews by Birth, Justified by Faith**: Pivot from the historical narrative of the Antioch dispute into the theological core of the letter.
    *   *Literary Note*: 2:20 serves as the existential center of the passage, transitioning from collective theological argument to personal mystical union.

### III. The Defense of the Covenant Promise (3:1-4:31)
*   **3:1-5 — The Galatians' Experience of the Spirit**: Rhetorical questions asking if they received the Spirit by *erga nomou* or by the hearing of faith.
*   **3:6-14 — The Abrahamic Covenant and the Curse of the Law**: Abraham believed and it was reckoned to him; those of faith are sons of Abraham. The Law brings a curse, but Christ redeemed us from the curse of the Law.
    *   *Literary Note*: A complex scriptural argument weaving Gen 15:6, Gen 12:3, Hab 2:4, Lev 18:5, and Deut 21:23.
*   **3:15-22 — The Law’s Subordinate Role**: The law, given 430 years later, does not annul the earlier promise. The law served as a guardian (*paidagōgos*) until Christ.
*   **3:23-29 — Sons and Heirs in Christ**: In Christ, there is no Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female; all are one.
*   **4:1-11 — From Slaves to Sons**: The transition from minor children under guardians to mature heirs.
*   **4:12-20 — Paul’s Pastoral Appeal**: Emotional plea recalling their warm reception of him.
*   **4:21-31 — The Allegory of Hagar and Sarah**: The two covenants representing slavery (Sinai / present Jerusalem) and freedom (heavenly Jerusalem).
    *   *Literary Note*: A typological/allegorical interpretation of Genesis 16 and 21, contrasting the son of the flesh with the son of the promise.
