## Linguistic Analysis & Etymological Exposition

### 1. Barnabas (Βαρναβᾶς)
The name *Barnabas* is a Hellenized form of a Semitic name (likely Aramaic).
*   **Semitic Roots**: 
    *   **Bar (בַּר)**: Aramaic noun meaning "son" (corresponding to the Hebrew *ben*).
    *   **Nabi / Nebuah (נְבִיא / נְבוּאָה)**: Aramaic and Hebrew roots for "prophet" or "prophecy".
*   **Linguistic Interpretation**: 
    The early church translated the name as "son of encouragement" (*yios paraklēseōs* in Greek). While the direct etymology refers to a "son of a prophet" or "son of prophecy," in the Semitic idiom, a "son of [attribute]" is one who embodies that characteristic. Prophecy in the New Testament is intimately tied to building up, encouraging, and consoling the church (cf. 1 Corinthians 14:3, where prophecy is defined as *oikodomēn kai paraklēsin kai paramuthian*—edification, encouragement, and consolation). 

---

### 2. Abigail (אֲבִיגַיִל / אֲבִיגָל)
The name *Abigail* is a classic Hebrew compound name.
*   **Hebrew Roots**:
    *   **Ab / Abi (אָב / אֲבִי)**: Hebrew noun for "father," with the possessive pronominal suffix *i* meaning "my father" or acting as a connective vowel in compound names.
    *   **Gil (גִּיל)**: Hebrew verb/noun meaning "to rejoice" or "joy/rejoicing."
*   **Linguistic Interpretation**:
    The name literally translates to "My father is joy" or "Father's joy." In Hebrew morpho-semantics, compound names starting with *Abi-* often express a theological assertion about God (e.g., "God is my father" or "My Father [God] is rejoicing") or represent a familial sentiment of delight at the birth of a daughter. Abigail in the narrative of 1 Samuel 25 exemplifies this joy through her quick wisdom and discretion, contrasting with her husband Nabal's foolishness.
