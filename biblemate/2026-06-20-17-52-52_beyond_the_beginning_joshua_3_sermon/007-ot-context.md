# Step 007: Historical and Cultural Context (Old Testament Context)

**Persona**: Oxford Bible Scholar
**Passage**: Joshua 3

This document examines the historical, geographical, and cultural background of the events recorded in Joshua 3. It places the crossing of the Jordan within the broader Near Eastern context of the Late Bronze Age/Early Iron Age transition.

---

## 1. The Geographical and Seasonal Setting
*   **Shittim to the Jordan**: Shittim (Acacia Grove) was Israel's last encampment east of the Jordan, located in the plains of Moab opposite Jericho. It was a site of previous failure (the rebellion at Baal-Peor, Numbers 25). The move from Shittim to the Jordan bank (v. 1) is a deliberate step away from a place of spiritual compromise toward the place of divine encounter.
*   **The Jordan in Flood Stage**: Joshua 3:15 contains a crucial parentheses: *"for the Jordan overflows all its banks all the days of harvest."* The harvest season refers to the barley harvest in the spring (March/April), which coincides with the melting snows of Mount Hermon feeding into the headwaters of the Jordan. In antiquity, before modern dams and water diversion projects, the Jordan River at this season would widen into a formidable, swift-moving torrent, muddy and deep, overflowing its lower valley (the *Ghor*). The river would be physically impassable for a large migration of families, livestock, and heavy equipment. By emphasizing this seasonal detail, the text highlights that the crossing was humanly impossible, eliminating any naturalistic explanation.

---

## 2. Leadership Transition and Legitimization
*   **Moses to Joshua**: The death of Moses left a massive leadership vacuum. Moses was the towering figure of the Exodus, the lawgiver, and the mediator of the covenant. Joshua, though a capable military commander (Exodus 17) and Moses' loyal assistant, needed divine validation in the eyes of the people.
*   **The exalting of Joshua**: YHWH tells Joshua, *"This day I will begin to exalt you in the sight of all Israel, that they may know that, as I was with Moses, so I will be with you"* (v. 7). The splitting of the Jordan is the direct counterpart to Moses' splitting of the Red Sea (Exodus 14). By replicating the exact miracle that initiated Moses' leadership, God establishes Joshua's authority. The Jordan crossing acts as a public coronation, legitimizing Joshua as Moses' true successor.

---

## 3. Ancient Near Eastern Ideology and River Crossings
*   **Rivers as Mythological Boundaries**: In Ancient Near Eastern mythology (e.g., Canaanite epic texts from Ugarit), rivers and seas were associated with chaos, death, and hostile deities (such as *Yamm*, the god of the sea and river chaos). A river in flood was a physical manifestation of chaos. The crossing of a major river was often viewed as a journey between realms—from the realm of the dead/wilderness to the realm of life/fertility.
*   **Yahweh's Cosmic Sovereignty**: When the Ark of the Covenant leads Israel into the flooding Jordan, YHWH is depicted as marching directly into the heart of chaos. By commanding the waters to stand in a "heap" (*nēḏ*, v. 13, 16), Yahweh demonstrates His absolute mastery over the forces of nature. The title used in v. 11 and v. 13—*’ăḏôn kol-hā’āreṣ* ("the Lord of all the earth")—is a polemic against Canaanite agricultural gods (like Baal). It asserts that YHWH is not a localized, nomadic desert deity, but the sovereign ruler of Canaan itself. The dry land crossing (*ḥārāḇâ*, v. 17) mirrors the Red Sea crossing (Exodus 14:21), showing that the same God who rescued them from Egypt is now planting them in Canaan.

---

## 4. The Ritual Procession
*   **The Ark as Vanguard**: Unlike military invasions where armed soldiers lead, the vanguard of Israel's advance is the Ark of the Covenant carried by priests (v. 6). The crossing is presented not as a military assault, but as a formal, liturgical procession. The distance of 2,000 cubits (about 3,000 feet) between the people and the Ark (v. 4) serves a dual purpose: it maintains the cultic boundary of holiness (the Ark was too sacred to be approached casually), and it allows the entire assembly to clearly see the Ark leading the way.
