# Step 006: Key Word Analysis (Exegesis and original languages)

**Persona**: Oxford Bible Scholar
**Passage**: Joshua 3

This document provides a detailed historical-grammatical and lexical analysis of key Hebrew terms in Joshua 3. The analysis focuses on semantic range, morphological parsing, and theological significance within the Ancient Near Eastern context.

---

## 1. Consecrate / Sanctify — קָדַשׁ (*qāḏaš*, Strong's H6942)
*   **Occurrence**: Joshua 3:5 — הִתְקַדָּשׁוּ (*hiṯqaddāšû*)
*   **Morphology**: Verb, Hitpael, Imperative, Masculine Plural.
*   **Semantic Range & Lexical Meaning**:
    The root *q-d-š* fundamentally denotes "separateness," "holiness," or "being set apart" from common, profane use for exclusive dedication to the divine. In the *Qal* stem, it means to be holy; in the *Piel* or *Hiphil*, to sanctify or declare holy.
    The use of the *Hitpael* (reflexive/iterative stem) in Joshua 3:5 is highly significant. It denotes a self-reflexive action: "sanctify yourselves" or "consecrate yourselves." It demands active, personal participation in preparing oneself for a holy encounter.
*   **Contextual & Theological Significance**:
    Within the Levitical system, consecration involved ritual washings, changing garments, and refraining from sexual relations (cf. Exodus 19:10-15). However, in the historical context of crossing the Jordan, this preparation is not merely external. It constitutes a psychological and spiritual realignment. The people are about to witness the raw, unmediated power of Yahweh. The *Hitpael* imperative indicates that holiness is not merely something God imposes, but a state the covenant community must actively enter through obedience and purification. It is the prerequisite for witnessing the *niplā’ôt* (wonders).

---

## 2. Cross over / Pass over — עָבַר (*‘āḇar*, Strong's H5674)
*   **Occurrence**: Used extensively throughout the chapter (v. 1, 3, 4, 6, 11, 14, 16, 17) in various verbal stems (primarily *Qal* and *Hiphil*).
*   **Morphology**: Primarily *Qal* Infinitive Construct לַעֲבֹר (*la‘ăḇōr* - "to pass over") and *Qal* Participle Active עֹבֵר (*‘ōḇēr* - "passing over").
*   **Semantic Range & Lexical Meaning**:
    The root *‘-ḇ-r* refers to the physical action of crossing a boundary, passing through a territory, or traversing a body of water. It is the root from which the term "Hebrew" (*‘Iḇrî*) is etymologically derived (signifying "those from beyond [the river]").
*   **Contextual & Theological Significance**:
    In Joshua 3, *‘āḇar* is the thematic engine of the narrative. It represents the transition from the liminal wilderness space (associated with testing, wandering, and the old generation's failure) to the structured land of inheritance (the fulfillment of the Abrahamic covenant). The repetition of *‘āḇar* underscores the physical and theological reality of crossing. It is not a metaphorical crossing; it is a concrete, historical act where the entire nation physically enters the sphere of the promise.

## 3. Ark — אָרוֹן (*’ārôn*, Strong's H727)
*   **Occurrence**: Mentioned 17 times in this chapter alone, showing its literary centrality.
*   **Syntactic Constructs**:
    *   *’ărôn berîṯ-YHWH* (the ark of the covenant of Yahweh) — v. 3
    *   *’ărôn habberîṯ* (the ark of the covenant) — v. 6, v. 8, v. 14
    *   *’ărôn habberîṯ ’ăḏôn kol-hā’āreṣ* (the ark of the covenant of the Lord of all the earth) — v. 11
*   **Semantic Range & Lexical Meaning**:
    An *’ārôn* is a chest, coffer, or box. In secular contexts, it could refer to a money chest (2 Kings 12:10) or a coffin (Genesis 50:26). In the cultic sphere of Israel, it refers exclusively to the gold-plated acacia chest containing the tablets of the Decalogue (Deuteronomy 10:1-5).
*   **Contextual & Theological Significance**:
    The Ark is the supreme symbol of the localized, active presence of Yahweh. It serves as His footstool (Psalm 132:7) and the place where He enthrones Himself between the cherubim (Exodus 25:22). In Joshua 3, the Ark is described as "passing over before you" (v. 11). By entering the Jordan first, Yahweh Himself takes the vanguard. He goes into the place of chaos (represented by the flooding Jordan) to secure the passage for His people. The description of YHWH as "Lord of all the earth" (*’ăḏôn kol-hā’āreṣ*) in connection with the Ark asserts His absolute sovereignty over all territorial domains, directly challenging the localized territorial claims of the Canaanite pantheon.

## 4. Know — יָדַע (*yāḏa‘*, Strong's H3045)
*   **Occurrence**: Joshua 3:7 — יֵדְעוּן (*yēḏe‘ûn*); Joshua 3:10 — תֵּדְעוּן (*tēḏe‘ûn*)
*   **Morphology**: Verb, Qal, Imperfect, 3rd Person Masculine Plural / 2nd Person Masculine Plural with paragogic nun (adding emphasis).
*   **Semantic Range & Lexical Meaning**:
    *   *yāḏa‘* designates relational, experiential, and covenantal knowledge. It is distinct from purely intellectual or speculative comprehension. To "know" YHWH is to experience His active intervention, to acknowledge His lordship, and to submit to His covenant demands.
*   **Contextual & Theological Significance**:
    In v. 7, the goal of exalting Joshua is so that the people may "know" that YHWH is with him as He was with Moses. In v. 10, the miracle itself is the mechanism by which they will "know that the living God (*’ēl ḥay*) is among you." The knowledge of God is thus grounded in historical, empirical, and supernatural acts. The presence of the "living God" is contrasted with the dead, silent idols of the nations. It is a knowledge forged in the crucible of active faith and visible deliverance.

---

## 5. Wonders / Miracles — פָּלָא (*pālā’*, Strong's H6381)
*   **Occurrence**: Joshua 3:5 — נִפְלָאוֹת (*niplā’ôṯ*)
*   **Morphology**: Verb, Niphal, Participle Feminine Plural (functioning as a substantive noun).
*   **Semantic Range & Lexical Meaning**:
    The root *p-l-’* denotes that which is extraordinary, hard to comprehend, or supernatural. In the *Niphal*, it refers to the wondrous deeds of God—miraculous interventions in history that transcend human capability and natural law (cf. Exodus 3:20; Psalm 78:11).
*   **Contextual & Theological Significance**:
    The *niplā’ôṯ* are the signature of Yahweh's covenantal activity. When Joshua promises that YHWH will do "wonders among you" (v. 5), he is linking the impending Jordan crossing with the grand narrative of the Exodus plagues and the parting of the Red Sea. The *Niphal* participle indicates that these wonders are not random anomalies, but purposeful, divine disclosures of power designed to bring about redemptive ends.
